Today’s question: When you claim X causes Y, how do you prove it?

Why Causal Arguments Matter

In Ethiopia, causal claims come up every day in MNH work:

  • A consultant tells an Ethiopian minister: “The vacancy rate at health posts is causing preventable newborn deaths.”
  • A report states: “Community Health Extension Workers improved antenatal care coverage by 18% in Ethiopia.”
  • A donor asks: “How do you know this program in Ethiopia caused the improvement?”

Each of these is a causal claim. And every one of them can be challenged.

The Core Problem

Correlation Is Not Causation

Correlation vs. Causation

Correlation

In Ethiopia, woredas with more Community Health Extension Workers tend to have lower maternal mortality.

The two move together.

Causation

Deploying CHEWs to underserved woredas in Ethiopia caused maternal mortality to decline.

One produced the other.

Correlation tells you two things move together. Causation tells you one made the other happen.

Stakeholders in Ethiopia need to know which claim is being made.

The Timeline Trap

The most common causal mistake:

Because X happened before Y, X must have caused Y.

  • A CHEW training program launched in Ethiopia in January
  • Neonatal mortality dropped by March
  • Therefore the program caused the drop. Right?
  • Not necessarily. Timing alone is not proof.

. . .

Example

A new CHEW training program was rolled out in Ethiopia’s Tigray region in Q1. Neonatal mortality dropped 12% by Q3. But Ethiopia’s national bed-net distribution campaign also ran during the same period.

Did the training cause the drop — or did fewer malaria-related neonatal deaths?

Three Things to Rule Out

Before you claim X caused Y

The Three Alternatives

1. Common Cause
Could something else cause both X and Y?

▲

2. Coincidence
Could X and Y be unrelated?

X caused Y?

3. Reversed Causation
Could Y actually cause X?
▼

Your Causal Claim
Only holds if you rule out all three

1 Common Cause

Could a third factor be causing both X and Y?

  • In Ethiopia, we can observe: woredas with more NGO programs also have better MNH outcomes
  • But both might be driven by a third factor: government prioritization under Ethiopia’s HSTP
  • High-priority woredas in Ethiopia attract more NGOs and receive more government investment

Example

Your claim: “Our safe delivery kit deployment reduced obstetric complications in Ethiopia.”

The challenge: The health centers in Ethiopia chosen for deployment were already higher-performing because they had stronger woreda health office leadership — the common cause.

2 Coincidence

Could X and Y just happen to move together without any real connection?

  • The more data points tracked across Ethiopia, the more spurious correlations emerge
  • Especially in small samples (a single health center, one quarter of data)
  • Ask yourself: is there a plausible mechanism connecting X and Y?

Example

A health center in Ethiopia’s SNNPR introduced a new patient register the same month neonatal mortality dropped. Did the register cause the drop?

More likely: the drop was within normal quarterly variation. No mechanism connects a register format to newborn survival.

3 Reversed Causation

Could the direction of causation be backwards?

  • Consider this from Ethiopia: health posts with more supportive supervision visits have better outcomes
  • But maybe better-performing health posts attract more visits
  • The outcome might be driving the input, not the other way around

Example

Your claim: “More frequent supportive supervision visits improved health post performance in Ethiopia’s Oromia region.”

The challenge: Maybe supervisors visit high-performing health posts in Ethiopia more often because they are easier to reach and more cooperative. The performance is driving the visits — not the reverse.

The Checklist

Before claiming X caused Y in Ethiopia, run through these three checks:

Challenge The Question In Ethiopia’s Health System
Common Cause Could something else cause both? Check for confounders — Ethiopia’s HSTP priority status, geography, baseline capacity, donor attention
Coincidence Is the connection real or noise? Look for a plausible mechanism. Check sample sizes across Ethiopian woredas. Is this within normal variation?
Reversed Causation Is the direction backwards? Could the outcome be driving the input? Watch for selection effects in Ethiopia’s health system

Building a Causal Argument

From claim to conviction

The Two-Part Structure

A solid causal argument has two parts:

  • 1 Show the correlation — X and Y move together (use data from Ethiopia)
  • 2 Tell the causal story — explain how X produces Y (the mechanism)

The causal story is what separates a correlation from a cause. It is a chain of reasoning that explains the steps between X and Y.

Remember “Why It’s True” from the Anatomy framework? The causal story is your “why it’s true” for causal claims.

What Makes a Good Causal Story

Bad

“We deployed CHEWs in Ethiopia and maternal mortality dropped.”

Correlation only. No mechanism.

Good

“Ethiopia’s CHEWs conducted home visits → identified danger signs early → referred mothers to health centers faster → reduced the first and second delays in emergency obstetric care → reduced maternal hemorrhage deaths.”

Chain of mechanism.

A good causal story traces the chain of events step by step. Each link should be plausible and, ideally, supported by evidence.

The Removal Test

The strongest test of causation: if you removed X, would Y still happen?

  • If you pulled Ethiopia’s CHEWs out, would the improvements vanish?
  • If you stopped the mentorship program in Ethiopia, would facility performance decline?
  • If yes — you have strong evidence of causation

Example

We paused the essential medicine supply chain reform for one quarter in control woredas in Ethiopia. Stockout rates climbed back to baseline. We reinstated it. Stockouts dropped again.

That is evidence of causation — not just correlation.

Putting It All Together

Full Example

1 The Claim: “Our mentorship program reduced neonatal mortality in target health centers in Ethiopia’s Amhara region.”

2 The Correlation: “Health centers in Ethiopia with mentored midwives saw a 35% drop in neonatal deaths over 12 months; matched control facilities saw no change.”

3 The Causal Story: “Mentored midwives in Ethiopia learned neonatal resuscitation skills, applied them in delivery rooms, recognized danger signs earlier, and initiated treatment faster.”

4 Ruling Out Alternatives: “Not common cause (control facilities in Ethiopia had similar baseline resources). Not coincidence (35% drop is well beyond normal quarterly variation). Not reversed causation (facilities were randomly assigned, not self-selected).”

5 The Removal Test: “When mentors rotated out of two Ethiopian facilities temporarily, neonatal resuscitation rates dropped within weeks.”

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing correlation with causation — “The data from Ethiopia shows X and Y are linked” is not the same as “X caused Y”
  • Relying on timing alone — “It happened after our program launched in Ethiopia” is the timeline trap
  • Ignoring common causes — The most frequent failure in program evaluation in Ethiopia and globally
  • Skipping the causal story — If you can’t explain how X causes Y step by step, neither can your audience
  • Overclaiming from small samples — One health center in Ethiopia, one quarter of data is not enough to establish a pattern

Calibrate Your Language

Match your claim to the strength of your evidence:

Evidence Strength Appropriate Language
Strong RCT or quasi-experimental design “X caused Y”
Consistent correlation + plausible mechanism “X likely contributed to Y”
Observed correlation, mechanism unclear “X is associated with Y”
Single case, limited data “X may have played a role in Y”
No mechanism, timing only “We observed Y after X, but cannot yet attribute causation”

Calibrate your language. Stakeholders in Ethiopia and beyond respect precision. Overclaiming destroys credibility.

Key Takeaways

  1. Correlation is not causation — showing X and Y move together is step one, not the finish line
  2. Rule out the three alternatives — common cause, coincidence, reversed causation
  3. Tell the causal story — trace the mechanism step by step
  4. Apply the removal test — if X disappears, does Y?
  5. Calibrate your claim — match your language to your evidence

A causal argument that holds up does three things: shows the pattern, explains the mechanism, and rules out the alternatives.
